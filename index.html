ℹ️ Why Punjabi Matters

The story behind this project — and why learning Punjabi is an act of love.

📊 The Numbers

Punjabi is spoken by over 125 million people worldwide, making it one of the top 10 most spoken languages on earth. In Indian Punjab, it is the sole official language and is the mother tongue of around 90% of the state's population. Punjab's literacy rate stands at approximately 77.5% as of 2025 — above India's national average — but that number measures the ability to read any language. Reading and writing Punjabi in Gurmukhi script is a different story entirely.

⚠️ The Quiet Crisis

Despite Punjabi being the official language of Indian Punjab, it has faced systematic displacement for over a century — first under British colonial rule, when Urdu was imposed as the language of courts and administration, and later through independent India's push toward Hindi as a national language.

Today the threat comes from within. Private schools across Punjab — even in small towns — openly discourage or ban students from speaking Punjabi on campus, treating English and Hindi as markers of sophistication. Punjabi has been labelled ਪੇਂਡੂ (pendu — rustic, village-level) by urban Punjabis themselves. A 2024 academic study classified Punjabi as an endangered language using UNESCO's Language Vitality framework, noting it scores poorly on institutional support and intergenerational transmission.

While the Punjab Learning of Panjabi Act (2008, amended 2021) legally mandates Punjabi be taught in all schools from Class 1 to 10, enforcement is weak. Many private schools find workarounds, and the unwritten culture in many institutions actively punishes Punjabi use. Across the border in Pakistan, Punjabi has never been an official language despite being spoken by the majority — Urdu dominates all formal schooling there.

🌍 The Diaspora's Role

There are large Punjabi diaspora communities in Canada, the UK, the United States, Australia, and beyond — with Canada alone home to over 800,000 Punjabi speakers. For many second and third-generation diaspora kids, Punjabi exists in the in-between: they grew up hearing it at home, at the gurdwara, at weddings — but were never taught to read or write it. The spoken language slowly erodes too as English becomes the default.

This matters because language is not just a communication tool — it carries an entire worldview. The poetry of Bulleh Shah, the verses of Guru Granth Sahib, the folk songs of Punjab, the wit and warmth embedded in Punjabi proverbs — all of it becomes inaccessible when the script is lost. You can love Karan Aujla's music without understanding a word; but when you understand it, something deeper opens up.

Ironically, some of the most passionate Punjabi language preservation work is now happening outside Punjab — in diaspora gurdwaras in Birmingham and Brampton, in online communities, and in small projects like this one. The diaspora may end up being the ones who keep the language alive.

🌾 Why Learn to Write, Not Just Speak

Many diaspora Punjabis speak conversationally but cannot read Gurmukhi. This creates a one-way relationship with the language — you can receive it, but you can't fully inhabit it. Learning to read and write Gurmukhi script means you can:

  • Read the Guru Granth Sahib in its original script
  • Understand Punjabi poetry, literature, and song lyrics as intended
  • Write your name, a letter to a grandparent, or a message in your mother tongue
  • Pass something real on to your own children
  • Connect with a literary tradition stretching back over 500 years

✨ About This Project

ਰੂਹ ਦੀ ਬੋਲੀ — Language of the Soul — was built for diaspora learners who want a structured, modern, and genuinely enjoyable way to reconnect with the language. The curriculum draws on the framework of classic Punjabi textbooks, taught through the culture diaspora kids already love: songs, movies, slang, and stories. No boring drills. No shame. Just ਪਿਆਰ (love) for the language.
